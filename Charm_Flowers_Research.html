Project Overview

From product browsing to meaningful gifting.

The original experience focused on displaying flower products. The redesign reframes the journey around user intent, emotional context, and guided decision-making — transforming a catalog into a confidence-building tool.

The Problem
Users struggled to choose the right bouquet for the right moment.
Too many options, weak guidance, and unclear flower symbolism created decision fatigue and abandoned carts — not because users didn't want to buy, but because they didn't feel confident choosing.
Business Context
High drop-off at the product selection stage was hurting conversion.
Analytics showed 62% of sessions ended at the browse/select phase. Improving the selection experience was a direct revenue opportunity — especially around key gifting occasions (Valentine's Day, Birthdays, Mother's Day).
The Goal
Make flower selection feel simple, personal, and emotionally relevant.
Guide users from emotional intent to confident purchase in fewer steps — without stripping away the richness and meaning that makes flowers special as a gift.

✓Large catalogs (50+ products) increased abandonment, not conversion.
✓Personalization felt important, but users wanted guardrails, not full creative control.
✓Price uncertainty (add-ons, delivery) caused last-minute abandonment at checkout.
✓Mobile experience felt misaligned with emotional gifting context.

UX Rationale — Why Emotion-First Works
✓
Reduces cognitive loadStarting with an emotional anchor (rather than product type) dramatically narrows the decision space — from 60+ products to 3–5 curated options.
✓
Mirrors real buying motivationResearch showed 72% of users cared about the meaning behind flowers — aligning the interface with this mental model felt natural and trustworthy.
✓
Supports both buyer typesEmotional buyers get guided validation. Practical buyers get speed — they can reach checkout in under 3 taps with no emotional deliberation required.
✓
Creates differentiationCompetitors lead with product categories. Leading with emotion positions FlowersBuilder as the thoughtful, human-centered alternative.

UX Strategy Thinking

The principles behind the decisions.

Beyond execution, this case study reflects a clear strategic philosophy: emotional commerce requires emotional design thinking — not just better UI.

01
Reduce to Relate
Fewer choices, presented with meaning, build more confidence than comprehensive catalogs. The strategic decision to narrow SKU visibility on first interaction was counter-intuitive — but research-backed and measurably effective.
02
Design for the Moment, Not the Product
Flower buyers are not shopping — they're trying to express something. Designing around the emotional occasion rather than the product catalog changes the entire information architecture and copywriting philosophy.
03
Progressive Disclosure as Care
Not every user wants to customize deeply. Progressive disclosure respects the practical buyer's time while offering depth to the emotional buyer. The default experience should work beautifully for everyone — not just power users.

Reflection

What I would do next.

To move the product further, I would test AI-assisted bouquet recommendations using past purchase behavior, run A/B tests on emotion-first vs. occasion-first entry points, and connect post-purchase feedback (did the gift land well?) back into the recommendation model to improve personalization over time.
