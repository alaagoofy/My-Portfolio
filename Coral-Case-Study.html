What is Coral

A digital marketplace for bundled healthcare services — connecting employers, care managers, and providers through direct contracting, shared workflows, and a single communication layer.

The Problem

The live platform scored 49.9 on SUS — Class F. Patients, providers, and care managers navigated fragmented, disconnected screens with no unified channel, causing care delays and lost provider payments.

The Solution

Coral unifies Employers, Employees, Care Managers, and Medical Service Providers on one platform — enabling direct bundle contracting, secure messaging, and frictionless payment flows under a single digital experience.

My role extended beyond executing screens. I led the design strategy from a blank slate — aligning cross-functional stakeholders, setting the research agenda, driving ideation workshops, and owning quality through prototype validation. Every decision was grounded in user evidence and measured against business outcomes.

Design Strategy

Setting Direction Before
a Single Pixel

Before the team opened Figma, I aligned stakeholders on what success looked like. Without a shared definition, every design decision becomes a debate. With one, the team moves fast and in the same direction.

Executive Intent

Coral needed to evolve from a fragmented referral tool into a networked healthcare marketplace — connecting all four stakeholder types through shared workflows, transparent pricing, and a single communication layer. The vision I set: one platform that feels native to every user type, regardless of their role.

Success Criteria — Agreed With Stakeholders Before Design Began

  • Employers connect directly with Care Managers — no intermediary friction
  • Care Managers surface transparent bundle pricing before recommending
  • Patients receive care with zero hidden fees or coordination burden
  • Providers get paid reliably, tracking payments in the same system
  • All parties communicate through one channel — not across email, phone, and app

Design Philosophy I Established for the Team

Coral was a transformation — not a greenfield. Innovation discovers solutions to new problems. Transformation fixes failing ones. That distinction mattered: before proposing anything, we had to understand the root causes of failure in the existing system. I made this the team's first principle.

Platforms in scope: Web · Android · iOS

Four User Segments — One Shared Design Language

The core design challenge: four user types with different mental models, tasks, and success metrics — all on one platform. My architectural mandate was to build a shared IA and interaction model, then apply role-based views on top — not build four separate products.

01

Stakeholder Interviews — Framing the Real Problem Space

I personally led interviews with clinical consultants and domain experts. The outputs: a mapped problem space, a competitive context brief, and clearly defined user segments — all aligned with product, engineering, and clinical leads before user research began. This prevented the team from spending cycles on the wrong problems.

02

Heuristic Evaluation — Knowing What Was Already Broken

I ran a two-stage heuristic evaluation using Nielsen & Molich's 10 principles — first myself, then with two independent UX experts in a blinded parallel review. Two-stage blind evaluation eliminated confirmation bias and produced a severity-rated issue backlog before a single new screen was proposed.

Critical violations found

Poor user control on the referral flow · No system feedback for multi-step actions · Appointment scheduling mismatched real-world mental models · Visual inconsistency across appointment types that undermined user trust throughout.

03

SUS Survey — Establishing a Measurable Baseline

I deployed the System Usability Scale across 10 participants on the live platform. This gave the team a hard number to design against and a clear improvement target (75+). Without a baseline, you can't prove the redesign worked — this was the data I'd bring back to stakeholders after testing.

Class F

Phase 1

Empathize — Getting Close
to Real Users

I ran both research tracks in parallel to maximise depth without extending the timeline. Designer 1 handled qualitative interviews; Designer 2 ran quantitative surveys. I designed both instruments, reviewed outputs, and synthesised findings into the shared insight layer that drove Define.

Track 1 — Qualitative Research (User Interviews)

I designed the interview guide and directed Designer 1 in facilitating semi-structured contextual interviews — both active and passive modes — with 4 Healthcare Professionals. I focused the guide on surfacing the gap between what users said they needed and what they actually did.

Sample questions from the interview guide I designed

Walk me through the last bundle request you received. · What is the single most difficult thing about providing a healthcare service? · What matters most when you can't find the right bundle? · Why do some patients prefer bundles over individual services?

Track 2 — Quantitative Research (Online Surveys)

Designer 2 deployed and analysed multiple online surveys. I defined the key questions based on hypotheses from the qualitative track and used the results to pressure-test assumptions before they became design decisions. Three findings directly shaped the architecture:

Phase 2

Define — Turning Research
Into a Shared Direction

The Define phase is where I exercised the most leadership. Raw research is noise until someone synthesises it into a direction the team can act on. I ran all synthesis sessions, led the persona workshop, and wrote the POV statements that governed every subsequent decision.

Empathy Mapping

I facilitated an empathy mapping session with both designers using the full research corpus — interview recordings, survey data, field notes. The four-quadrant review (Said / Did / Thought / Felt) surfaced the gap between what users articulated and what they actually experienced.

Key synthesis

Users felt confident when a bundle covered all their needs seamlessly — and anxious the moment they had to switch context mid-task. The biggest gap: users consistently said communication was critical, yet the platform offered no unified communication layer whatsoever.

User Persona

I created 3 personas from research synthesis. These weren't portfolio decoration — they were decision tools. When the team disagreed on a direction, we asked: "which choice serves this persona's primary goal?" It resolved most debates in minutes.

👨‍💼

International Business background · Direct contracting veteran · Mid-40s · Driven by competence and independence

"To provide a successful service, your brand must be positioned where constructive expectations always meet reality."

  • Provide fair, end-to-end care with full provider visibility
  • Communicate with all parties in one place

Journey Mapping — Finding the Highest-Leverage Opportunities

I created current-state journey maps for each user segment, capturing scenario, needs, touchpoints, actions, emotions, and opportunities. These were working tools — used in team reviews and stakeholder presentations to build shared empathy around where to focus first.

Journey map finding → Architecture decision
Unify referral, messaging, and patient panel into one view
Care Managers navigated 3 separate screens to complete a single care coordination task. The journey map showed this as the deepest emotional low point. Fixing it became the most impactful structural decision in the redesign — and the one that accounted for the largest share of the SUS improvement.
Journey map finding → Feature decision
Embed payment tracking inside the referral context panel
Providers hit their lowest emotional point when chasing payment — they had no in-system visibility. Moving voucher and payment status into the referral panel meant providers never had to leave their primary workflow to check on payment.
Journey map finding → Notification design
Add proactive alerts for new patient requests
Care Managers reported missing new referrals because the system offered no proactive alerts. This became a high-priority feature — surfacing as an opportunity node at the journey map's most critical touchpoint.

Point of View — The Team's Design North Star

I synthesised all research into a two-row POV that became the team's shared decision compass. When a design call was contested, we returned here first.

UserNeedsThe Insight That Must Not Be Violated
Healthcare Professionals Effective, secure, and reliable correspondence across all parties — from the moment a patient requests care to when service is delivered. Time is the critical variable. Any delay in this chain damages patient outcomes and provider trust. The platform must eliminate coordination friction — not merely reduce it.
Patients Receive care that meets their needs and expectations, on time, without friction. Patients must never experience healthcare as a coordination problem. That burden belongs to the platform — not the user.
Phase 3

Ideate — Facilitating Sessions
That Actually Produce Decisions

I directed three ideation methods across the team. The goal wasn't a high volume of ideas — it was surfacing the non-obvious solutions that the evidence already pointed toward, then making fast decisions about which to pursue.

Method 1 — How Might We (HMW)

I facilitated HMW sessions with both designers to convert our POV problem statements into creative opportunity frames — open enough for unexpected solutions, narrow enough to stay grounded in the research.

Security & TrustHow might we make clients feel their information is safe — without adding onboarding friction?
CommunicationHow might we improve communication between all parties without building a separate messaging product?
AccessHow might we help clients and patients access the right bundle on the first attempt?
Decision QualityHow might we help patients choose the right service without a Care Manager explaining every option?
PaymentsHow might we accelerate provider payment without adding a chasing workflow?
Service DeliveryHow might we ensure patients receive the required service on time with zero manual coordination steps?
Method 2 — SCAMPER Analysis

I used SCAMPER to systematically challenge the team's attachment to existing UI patterns — specifically targeting the areas where the heuristic evaluation had found the most severe violations.

Method 3 — Crazy 8s (Team Sketching)

I ran Crazy 8s with both designers — 8 concepts per person in 8 minutes — then facilitated a team review, grouping concepts by theme and voting on strongest directions. The breakthrough came from the convergence of independent sessions.

The insight that changed everything

Three designers, three separate sessions — all three independently sketched collapsing the referral list, bundle panel, and messaging into a single context-aware layout. That convergence was more convincing than any single designer's proposal. As the lead, my job was to recognise it and commit. This became the architectural foundation of the entire redesign.

Phase 4

Prototype — Architecture
to Validated Design

I directed both designers through a three-stage prototyping process. At each gate, I reviewed work against the POV statements and journey maps — ensuring fidelity to the research, not just visual quality.

Stage 1 — Sketches & Lo-Fi

Starting with hand-drawn Crazy 8s, we moved into digital lo-fi wireframes. I reviewed variant explorations from both designers and made structural decisions before committing to higher fidelity — preventing cycles of wasted effort on visual polish before the layout was validated.

Stage 2 — High Fidelity

Hi-fi was built by both designers under my direction, with weekly critiques reviewing against the research. I made final calls on interaction patterns. Six key screens delivered:

01
Referrals List

Filterable and sortable with real-time status indicators — replacing a flat, unordered list

02
Unified Messaging View

Threaded conversations + persistent patient context — eliminating the 3-screen navigation that was the platform's biggest pain point

03
Streamlined Referral Creation

3-step flow with inline validation and ~35% fewer fields than the original

04
Patient Record View

Patient info, referral history, and communication in one panel — zero context switching

05
Bundle Marketplace

Searchable by category, price, and location with quality signals surfaced before selection

06
Voucher & Payment Tracking

Payment status embedded in the referral panel — providers no longer leave the referral to track payment

Single most impactful change

Unifying referral detail, patient information, and messaging into one context-aware panel. In the old design: 3 screens, 3 navigation steps per care coordination task. In the new design: zero navigation — everything in the same view. This single architectural decision accounted for the largest share of the 29-point SUS improvement.

Phase 5

Test — Validating the Design
With Real Users

Once the interactive prototype was ready, I evaluated the design with real users to confirm the solution met their expectations — and to surface any remaining usability or experience issues before committing to development. This phase closed the measurement loop I opened at the start with the SUS baseline.

Primary Method

Usability Testing

I used usability testing to directly investigate user behaviour as participants interacted with the prototype — observing where they hesitated, where they succeeded, and where the design failed their mental model. I ran both moderated and unmoderated formats to capture breadth and depth of findings.

🎙️
Moderated Remote Usability Testing

Conducted via online interview and direct observation — allowing me to ask follow-up questions in real time and probe the reasoning behind user actions as they happened.

🎥
Unmoderated Remote Usability Testing

Session recordings supported by eye tracking and/or follow-up surveys — capturing natural, uninfluenced behaviour at scale without a facilitator in the room.

Additional Testing Techniques

Alongside usability testing, I used three complementary methods to collect quantitative data and analytics that validated — or challenged — what usability sessions surfaced qualitatively.

⚖️
A/B Testing

Tested multiple versions of key screens simultaneously to determine which variant produced the strongest outcomes — removing opinion from layout and copy decisions.

🔥
Heat Mapping

Visualised how users moved around each page — identifying the most and least interacted areas, surfacing hidden friction points invisible to qualitative observation alone.

📋
Surveys

Gathered structured user feedback post-session to quantify satisfaction, capture sentiment on specific flows, and validate whether the redesign resolved the pain points identified in research.

Closing the Measurement Loop

The final usability test re-administered the SUS scale across participants to validate the improvement against the 49.9 baseline I established at the start of the project. The result exceeded the 75+ target I had set with stakeholders.

What the test results confirmed

The unified referral + messaging + patient panel — the architectural decision that came from Crazy 8s convergence — produced the largest individual improvement in task completion rate and confidence ratings. Users no longer navigated between screens to complete care coordination tasks. The design had solved the problem we'd identified in the journey map nine weeks earlier.

Reflections

What I'd Apply
to the Next Challenge

Not generic UX lessons — specific things I learned leading this team and project that changed how I approach design leadership.

01

Baseline data is your authority in the room

The SUS score of 49.9 was more persuasive than any design argument I could have made. It gave me the authority to push for full transformation when stakeholders preferred minor iteration. Without a number, you're debating opinions. With one, you're debating evidence. I now treat baseline measurement as the non-negotiable first step on any engagement.

02

The best decisions emerge from the team — your job is to create the conditions

The unified panel layout emerged from a Crazy 8s session I facilitated — not from my own sketching. Three designers independently converged on the same pattern. That convergence was far more convincing than if I'd imposed the idea myself. As a lead, my job is to create conditions where the right answer can surface — and recognise it when it does.

03

Resolve architecture before opening Figma

The decision to unify referral, patient, and messaging context had more impact than every visual design choice combined. When leading a team, I now insist on resolving structural decisions before visual work begins. Changing IA after screens are in production wastes everyone's effort and kills momentum.

04

Multi-user-type platforms need a shared language — not separate experiences

Designing for 4 user types meant building a shared IA and interaction model first, then applying role-based views on top. The temptation is to build four tailored experiences. The right answer is one coherent platform that respects each user's mental model. That architectural principle kept the team's work unified and the codebase maintainable.
